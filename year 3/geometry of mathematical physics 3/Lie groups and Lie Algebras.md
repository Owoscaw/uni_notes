
# Motivating examples:

## $U(1)$:
The group $U(1)$ derives its name from unitary complex $1\times1$ matrices. Acting with $g$ on a complex number $c$ we define a map:$$\Huge z\rightarrow gz,\,\,g\in U(1),\,\,z\in\mathbb{C}$$where we require that the inner form $|z|^2$ remains unchanged as:$$\Huge |z|^2=\bar zz\rightarrow\bar z\bar ggz=|g|^2|z|^2$$which implies that $g$ is a complex number of modulus one ($|g|^2=1$), therefore we can write $g=e^{i\varphi}$. Hence $U(1)$ is the group of complex numbers of unit modulus with multiplication as the group operation. We can prove this using the definition of a group. $g=1$ acts as the identity element for this group. The product of two complex numbers with unit modulus also has unit modulus, so we satisfy $x,y\in G\implies x\circ y\in G$. Complex numbers are associative under multiplication, so we satisfy associativity automatically. For $g=e^{i\varphi}$ we write $g^{-1}=e^{-i\varphi}$ and we see that $g\circ g^{-1}=e^{i\varphi}e^{-i\varphi}=e^0=1$, satisfying the inverse requirement for a group. Hence $U(1)$ is indeed a group under multiplication. We saw that a map from $\Re$ to $U(1)$ by writing $g=e^{i\varphi}$ is not an isomorphism. That is, we cannot simply do calculus on $\Re$ and map that to $U(1)$, this is an example of a manifold. When $\varphi\to0$ we can approximate the exponential map to linear order to get:$$\Huge z\rightarrow(1+i\varphi)z$$Note that this approximation is tangent to $U(1)$ at $g=1$. We can try to reconstruct finite elements of $U(1)$ by repeated infinitesimal transformations. Considering $(1+i\varphi/n)^n$ as our map, we see that as $n\to\infty$ the map is reconstructed. More formally:$$\Huge\begin{align}
\lim_{n\to\infty}(1+i\varphi/n)^n&=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{(i\varphi)^k}{n^k}{n\choose k}\\
&=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{(i\varphi)^k}{n^k}\frac{n!}{(n-k)!k!}\\
&=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{(i\varphi)^k}{k!}=e^{i\varphi}
\end{align}$$where we have used the fact that the term $\frac{n!}{(n-k)!n^k}$ will tend to one in the limiting case.

## $SU(2)$
We now aim to generalise the above notions to matrices, particularly $M_n(\mathbb{C})$. To do this we must recall [[Index notation|Einstein's summation notation]] as well as the Hermitian conjugate $(A^\dagger)_{ij}=(\bar A)_{ji}$.  We can write the trace and determinant of a matrix using index notation:$$\Huge\begin{align}
\det A&=\epsilon_{i_1i_2,\dots,i_n}A_{1i_1}A_{2i_2}\dots A_{ni_n}\\
\text{Tr }A&=A_{ii}
\end{align}$$where $\epsilon$ is the Levi-Civita symbol, a completely antisymmetric tensor. We can then define the general unitary groups:$$\Huge\begin{align}
U(n)&=\{g\in M_n(\mathbb{C}):g^\dagger g=gg^\dagger=\mathbb{1}\}\\
SU(n)&=\{g\in U(n):\det g=1\}
\end{align}$$Focusing on $SU(2)$ we can check if this is indeed a group wrt matrix multiplication:$$\Huge\begin{align}
e&=\mathbb{1}\implies g\mathbb{1}=\mathbb{1}g=g,\,\,\forall g\in SU(2)\\
\mathbb{1}\mathbb{1}^\dagger&=\mathbb{1}^\dagger\mathbb{1}=\mathbb{1}\implies e\in\ SU(2)\\
g,g'\in SU(2)&\implies(gg')^\dagger gg'=g'^\dagger g^\dagger gg'=g'^\dagger\mathbb{1}g'=\mathbb{1}\\
\det(gg')&=\det g\deg g'=1\times1=1\\
g\in SU(2)&\implies (g^{-1})^\dagger g^{-1}=(g^\dagger)^\dagger g^{-1}=gg^{-1}=1\\
\det(g^{-1})&=\frac{1}{\det g}=1
\end{align}$$And as matrix multiplication is associative, we have that $SU(2)$ is indeed a group. Note that we can think of $U(2)$ as the group of transformations $U$ on $\mathbb{C}^2$, $\underline z\rightarrow U\underline z=\underline w$, which will leave the inner product $|\underline z|$ invariant:$$\Huge |\underline z|=\begin{pmatrix}\bar z_1&\bar z_2\end{pmatrix}\mathbb{1}\begin{pmatrix}z_1\\z_2\end{pmatrix}=\underline z^\dagger\cdot\underline z$$so when we apply the transformation:$$\Huge \begin{align}
\underline z&\rightarrow U\underline z\\
\underline z^\dagger&\rightarrow(U\underline z)^\dagger=\underline z^\dagger U^\dagger\\
\underline z^\dagger\underline z&\rightarrow \underline z^\dagger U^\dagger U\underline z=\underline z^\dagger\underline z
\end{align}$$so we see that the inner product is indeed unchanged. We can also take the notion of parametrisation we saw in $U(1)$ to $U(2)$. 

For any invertible matrix in $M_2(\mathbb{C})$ we can write:$$\Huge g=\begin{pmatrix}a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix}\implies g^{-1}=\frac{1}{\det g}\begin{pmatrix}d & -b \\ -c & a\end{pmatrix}$$Now as $g\in SU(2)$ we have $\det g=1$ and $g^{-1}=g^\dagger$, so:$$\Huge \begin{pmatrix}d & -b \\ -c & a\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\bar a & \bar c \\ \bar b & \bar d\end{pmatrix}$$So the most general matrix in $SU(2)$ can be written as:$$\Huge g=\begin{pmatrix}a & b \\ -\bar b & \bar  a\end{pmatrix}$$now $\det g=1\implies|a|^2+|b|^2=1$. As these are complex numbers, we write $a=x_1+ix_2,b=x_3+ix_4$, so we have:$$\Huge SU(2)=\{\underline{x}\in\Re^4:x_1^2+x_2^2+x_3^2+x_4^2=1\}$$This is the defining equation of the three sphere. That is, $SU(2)$ is a space that looks like a three sphere $S^3$ and we have defined an embedding of $S^3$ into $\Re^4$.

### Pauli matrices:
When working with $U(1)$ we saw that we could write the group using the complex exponential. We aim to introduce a similar notion by writing:$$\Huge g=e^{iA}$$where $A$ is a matrix and $g\in SU(2)$. The exponential of a matrix has to be defined using the series definition:$$\Huge e^{iA}=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(iA)^k}{k!}$$Firstly we impose unitarity ($g^\dagger=g^{-1}$), as $(A^n)^\dagger=(A^\dagger)^n$ we have:$$\Huge g^\dagger=e^{-iA^\dagger},\,\,g^{-1}=e^{-iA}\implies A^\dagger=A$$we call such matrices "Hermitian". We must now find the determinant of $e^{iA}$:$$\Huge\begin{align*}
\det g&=\det e^{iA}\\
&=\det\lim_{n\to \infty}\left(\mathbb{1}+\frac{iA}{n}\right)^n\\
&=\lim_{n\to \infty}\det\left(\mathbb{1}+\frac{iA}{n}\right)^n\\
&=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\det\left(\mathbb{1}+\frac{iA}{n}\right)\right)^n
\end{align*}$$The determinant can be written explicitly as:$$\Huge\det\left(\mathbb{1}+\frac{iA}{n}\right)=\left(1+\frac{iA_{11}}{n}\right)\left(1+\frac{iA_{22}}{n}\right)-\frac{i^2A_{21}A_{12}}{n^2}$$for $A\in M_2(\mathbb{C})$. Expanding this shows:$$\Huge\det\left(\mathbb{1}+\frac{iA}{n}\right)=1+\frac{i\,\text{tr}(A)}{n}+\mathcal{O}(n^{-2})$$where in the limiting case, all terms of order greater than $n^{-1}$ are subleading, so we have:$$\Huge\det g=\det e^{iA}=\lim_{n\to \infty}\left(1+\frac{i\text{ tr}(A)}{n}\right)^n=e^{i\text{ tr(A)}}$$The restriction of $\det g=1$ implies that this RHS term is $1$:$$\Huge e^{i\text{ tr}(A)}=1\implies i\text{ tr}(A)=0\implies\text{tr}(A)=0$$Writing $g=e^{iA}$ implies that $A\in T$ where:$$\Huge T=\{A:A^\dagger=A,\text{ tr}(A)=0\}$$note that for $A,B\in T$ we have $aA+bB\in T$ for $a,b\in\Re$. That is, $T$ is a [[Vector space definitions|vector space]] over the reals. We make the following choice of basis for $T$:$$\Huge\sigma_1=\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix},\,\,\sigma_2=\begin{pmatrix}0 & -i \\ i & 0\end{pmatrix},\,\,\sigma_3=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}$$Such matrices are known as the "Pauli matrices". One can show that these matrices satisfy:$$\Huge[\sigma_i,\sigma_j]=2i\sigma_{ijk}\sigma_k$$where $[a,b]$ is the [[Commutators and Uncertainty Principle|commutator]]. Note that $SU(2)$ is a non-abelian Lie group. We can form group elements of $SU(2)$ by exponentiating arbitrary real linear combinations of the Pauli matrices, that is:$$\Huge g(\underline{\alpha})=e^{i\alpha_j\sigma_j}=e^{i\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}}$$for $\underline{\alpha}=(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3)$. As $\alpha_k\sigma_k$ is traceless and Hermitian for any real $\underline{\alpha}$ we have that $g\in SU(2)$, so we get a group element for every vector $\underline{\alpha}\in\Re^3$. Note that this cannot be an injective map as $\Re^3$ is not the same as $S^3$.

We now aim to find the coordinates defined by $\alpha_k$ in order to connect the idea that $SU(2)\cong S^3$. First, consider:$$\Huge g((\alpha_1,0,0))=e^{i\alpha_1\sigma_1}=\sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{(i\alpha_1\sigma_1)^k}{k!}=\sum_{k=\text{even}}+\sum_{k=\text{odd}}$$now as $\sigma_i^2=\mathbb{1}$, all of the matrix powers in the sum over even $k$ equal $\mathbb{1}$ and all of the matrix powers in the odd sum equal $\sigma_1$:$$\Huge e^{i\alpha_1\sigma_1}=\sum_{k=\text{even}}\frac{(i\alpha_1)^k}{k!}\mathbb{1}+\sum_{k=\text{odd}}\frac{(i\alpha_1)^k}{k!}\sigma_1=\cos(\alpha_1)\mathbb{1}+i\sin(\alpha_1)\sigma_1$$This can be though of as a generalisation of Euler's formula. It is clear to see the non-injectivity of the map as $\alpha_1$ and $\alpha_1+2\pi$ will map to the same element. As $\sigma_1$ is self commutating:$$\Huge g((\alpha_1,0,0))g((\alpha_1',0,0))=e^{i\alpha_1\sigma_1}e^{i\alpha_1'\sigma_1}=g((\alpha_1+\alpha_1',0,0))$$so that the matrices of this form are a subgroup of $SU(2)$. Such subgroup works in the same way as $U(1)$ parametrised as $e^{i\phi}$ setting $\alpha_1=\phi$. More formally, these groups are [[Groups#Isomorphism|isomorphic]].

We propose that there is an injective group homomorphism $U(1)\rightarrow SU(2)$, the image of which is a $U(1)$ subgroup of $SU(2)$. One can show that this is true for any subsets of $\underline{\alpha}$ with $\underline{\alpha}=t\alpha_0$, so there are infinitely many $U(1)$ subgroups of $SU(2)$.

We define such subgroups $G_\alpha$ of $SU(2)$ as:$$\Huge G_\alpha=\{e^{it\alpha\sigma}:t\in\Re\}$$for some fixed $\alpha$. This is known as the one-parameter subgroup generated by $\alpha$. A nice property of our definition is that it is easy to find infinitesimal elements using a Taylor expansion. When $\underline{\alpha}$ is small, we write:$$\Huge g(\underline{\alpha})\approx\mathbb{1}+i\alpha_j\sigma_j=\mathbb{1}+i\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}$$That is, the space of tangent vectors at $g=\mathbb{1}$ is spanned by $i\sigma_j$ and a general vector is written as $i\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}$. $SU(2)$ can then be reconstructed by an infinite iteration of infinitesimal elements:$$\Huge g(\underline{\alpha})=\lim_{n\to \infty}\left(\mathbb{1}+\frac{i\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}}{n}\right)^n=e^{i\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}}$$Moreover we can think of recovering any infinitesimal generator $i\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}$ by considering the path:$$\Huge s(t)=e^{it\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}}$$through $SU(2)$ and taking a derivative wrt $t$ evaluated at $t=0$ (corresponding to $\mathbb{1}\in SU(2)$):$$\Huge \frac{\partial s(t)}{\partial t}|_{t=0}=\frac{\partial }{\partial t}e^{it\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}}|_{t=0}=i\underline{\alpha}\cdot\underline{\sigma}$$
